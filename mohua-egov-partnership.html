Partnership Proposal · Urban India

eGov Foundation & MoHUA —
a proposal for continued collaboration

eGov Foundation and MoHUA have worked together on urban digital governance for over a decade. Under MoHUA's leadership, eGov supported the architecture of DIGIT, which became the technical foundation for UPYOG — India's national urban governance platform, now fully owned and operated by MoHUA through NIUA, serving 4,400+ urban local bodies across 30 states.

Given MoHUA's current priorities and the policy context in 2026 — the launch of UCF, the 16th Finance Commission's urban grants, the new SWM Rules, and active work on NMAM 2.0 and RERA data standards — there are several areas where eGov can offer technical support. The six ideas below set out what that could look like in practice.

In each case, the framing is the same: MoHUA and its agencies determine the direction; eGov contributes technical capacity, platform infrastructure, and implementation support where it is useful.

Six proposals

Where eGov can support
MoHUA's urban agenda

Each proposal identifies an area where eGov's existing work and technical capacity could be useful to MoHUA's priorities. MoHUA and its agencies would lead and own each initiative. Click any card to read the full detail.

01
UCF Digital Operating System
UCF's operational guidelines require a centralised reform portal for tracking city reform roadmaps and triggering fund tranches. eGov can contribute technical architecture drawing from the NMAM 2.0 and DGM work already underway.
▾
The UCF guidelines mandate a centralised reform portal where cities submit roadmaps, report progress, and trigger tranche releases — but this portal has no designated builder yet. Fund releases require geo-tagged physical progress evidence, utilisation certificates, and verification by Independent Review and Monitoring Agencies. At the scale of 4,223 eligible cities across three verticals and five reform tracks, this is a significant digital infrastructure challenge. eGov's ongoing NMAM 2.0 and XVI FC DGM work has produced directly relevant system design thinking that could feed into MoHUA's approach here.
MoHUA's role Lead the design and ownership of the UCF programme management system through the UCF division and NIUA. Define the reform portal architecture, NAC workflows, and IRMA coordination framework. Operate and own the system long-term.
eGov's support Provide technical inputs on system architecture drawing from NMAM 2.0 and DGM work — reform milestone schema, UC generation from transactional data, tranche-release workflow design. Available to embed a technical team within MoHUA's programme unit.
Platform layer DIGIT infrastructure as a potential foundation. PFMS integration for fund flow verification. APIs to UPYOG for service delivery data and cityfinance.in for financial account status.
Policy layer NMAM 2.0 accounting standards inform the data schema. UCF reform tracks (Sections 8.1–8.5 of guidelines) define the milestone framework. XVI FC grant conditions provide the compliance baseline.
Why now → UCF operational guidelines were published April 2026. The reform portal is referenced but unbuilt. States must submit project proposals within 6 months. eGov's DGM workshops (April 7–9) produced system design thinking directly relevant to this.
02
UPYOG Adoption as UCF Reform Evidence
UCF requires cities to demonstrate digital governance reform, but no standards are currently in place to define what that means. eGov can support MoHUA and NIUA in designing an assessment framework based on UPYOG adoption metrics.
▾
Digital Governance is an admissible UCF project category, explicitly including digitisation of property records, utilities, and family IDs. UCF's reform framework requires "end-to-end digitisation of select municipal functions" as a governance reform condition — but the reform toolkit, which will define this standard, is yet to be issued. MoHUA and NIUA are well-placed to define UPYOG operational status (property tax, water billing, trade licences, grievance redressal live with transaction volume) as the measurable benchmark for this condition. Cities that already have it get reform credit; cities that don't can use it as their UCF Digital Governance project funded through the project preparation track. eGov can provide technical support in designing this maturity framework.
MoHUA's role Lead the design and notification of the Digital Governance Reform standard through the UCF reform toolkit. Embed UPYOG operational status in challenge-mode evaluation criteria at NAC. NIUA to coordinate with state teams on adoption pathways.
eGov's support Provide technical inputs on UPYOG module maturity levels (configured / live / transaction-generating / outcome-tracked). Support state onboarding through the 70-partner empanelled SI network. eGov Academy available for the individual capacity building the UCF guidelines require.
Platform layer UPYOG's 16 service modules as the implementation vehicle. DIGIT's open APIs allow state SIs to configure at scale. The maturity framework would be tracked within the UCF reform portal (Idea 1).
Policy layer UCF Section 8.2 (Governance & Digital Reforms) provides the policy hook. The UCF reform toolkit — to be issued separately per the guidelines — is the right vehicle for MoHUA to formalise this standard.
Why now → The UCF reform toolkit is listed as "to be issued separately." The design stage for that toolkit is now. A brief technical note from eGov to MoHUA/NIUA could contribute to its scope.
03
National Urban Governance Intelligence Layer
MoHUA's programme data sits in separate systems with no synthesis layer — e.g. AAINA for self-reported service benchmarks, UPYOG for live transactional service delivery data, MIS with administrative data on various schemes, and the upcoming UCF monitoring portal. eGov can support building an integration layer that gives the ministry and cities a single composite view at the ULB and state level.
▾
MoHUA currently has fragmented data systems covering different dimensions of city performance: cityfinance.in (financial accounts and rankings, built by Janaagraha), AAINA (self-reported service benchmarks), UPYOG (live transactional service delivery data), and the nascent UCF reform portal. No system synthesises across them. A governance intelligence layer that integrates these through APIs could give MoHUA, states, and cities a composite health view that doesn't exist today. This is not a competing portal — it would make existing portals more useful. A design conversation that includes Janaagraha, given their cityfinance.in ownership, would be the natural starting point.
MoHUA's role Determine whether a ministry-level governance intelligence view is a priority. If so, lead the convening of a design group with NIUA, Janaagraha, and technical partners. Enable API access across UPYOG/NIUA, AAINA, and the UCF reform portal. Own the platform and its public transparency layer.
eGov's support Contribute technical architecture inputs and the UPYOG data pipeline design. Support the API integration work given eGov's existing relationship with NIUA. Facilitate a design conversation with Janaagraha to ensure cityfinance.in data is integrated, not replicated.
Platform layer API integrations: UPYOG (transactional service data) + cityfinance.in (financial accounts) + AAINA (benchmarks) + UCF portal (reform trajectory). No new data collection — synthesis and presentation only.
Policy layer UCF's challenge-mode evaluation framework (Sections 7.2 A/B/C) provides the natural assessment structure. A ministry-owned governance intelligence platform would make UCF evaluation evidence-based rather than reliant on self-reporting.
Why now → UCF's National Apex Committee will need to evaluate city proposals across multiple data dimensions. The data sources to build a synthesis view already exist — the integration layer does not.
04
Ward-Level Citizen Engagement Layer
No existing system currently captures the citizen experience of service delivery at ward level. eGov has a working multilingual prototype and can support scaling it nationally under MoHUA's direction.
▾
Every existing data system in the urban governance stack — UPYOG, AAINA, cityfinance.in — captures government-generated data. None systematically captures citizen experience of service delivery at ward level. eGov has a working prototype of a ward-level citizen voice platform (developed for Bengaluru, multilingual) that captures grievances, service ratings, and local priority inputs — mapped to wards and cross-referenced with UPYOG service delivery records. If MoHUA sees value in a national citizen engagement standard, eGov can support the technical design and scaling of such a platform.
MoHUA's role Determine whether citizen engagement standards should be embedded in the UCF reform toolkit. Define what ward-level digital participation means as a governance reform indicator. Provide the policy framework for cities to adopt this as part of their reform roadmap.
eGov's support Share the Bengaluru prototype for MoHUA/NIUA's review. If taken forward, support scaling to a multi-city, multilingual platform and build the API connecting citizen signals to the governance intelligence layer (Idea 3).
Platform layer Ward-mapped feedback interface (multilingual, mobile-first). Cross-referencing with UPYOG service transaction data. Optional ICCC integration for real-time grievance routing — consistent with UCF's mandatory ICCC requirement for all projects.
Policy layer The 74th Constitutional Amendment mandates Ward Committees as participatory bodies — a digital citizen engagement platform operationalises that mandate. Citizen participation data could inform AAINA's benchmarking framework over time.
Why now → A working prototype already exists. The governance intelligence layer (Idea 3) benefits from a citizen data source. eGov can share the prototype with MoHUA/NIUA as a starting point for discussion.
05
Inter-Departmental Convergence for Climate-Resilient Cities
Cities are building climate action plans, which require significant inter-departmental coordination and overcoming information asymmetry. For example, SWM and air quality enforcement sit in separate departments with no shared data infrastructure, and urban heat strategies and support for street vendors are in separate silos. eGov can support MoHUA in building the connections that would make inter-departmental climate action practical.
▾
Effective urban climate action depends on data and regulatory authority that sits across multiple departments — SWM with ULBs, air quality with pollution control boards, heat risk and livelihoods with different line ministries. MoHUA is well-placed to convene this convergence. Two early-stage activations illustrate what this could look like:

SWM → Air Quality enforcement pipeline. Open waste burning is a significant source of PM2.5 in Indian cities. The Solid Waste Management Rules 2026 (in force from April 1, 2026) formally designate State Pollution Control Boards as enforcement authorities for SWM — but there is currently no data pipeline connecting SWM operations to these boards. A centralised national digital waste tracking portal is mandated within 6 months. eGov is already building SWM digital infrastructure on DIGIT with the Government of Karnataka (MoU with RDPR in place), covering waste classification, route optimisation, MRF management, and citizen grievance. This architecture could feed into the national portal design. Connecting it to CPCB's ambient air quality monitoring data creates a traceable SWM-to-air-quality accountability chain that enforcement authorities currently lack.

Parametric heat support for PM SVANidhi vendors. ● early stage Street vendors are among the most exposed urban populations to extreme heat events. Parametric financial support products — triggered by heat index thresholds rather than assessed claims — are being explored by climate finance partners as a way to provide rapid, verifiable income support during heat waves. MoHUA's existing SVANidhi digital infrastructure (vendor registration, geolocation, digital payment rails) provides a ready beneficiary identification and disbursement layer. eGov is in early-stage discussion with climate finance partners on this concept and could facilitate a design conversation with MoHUA and relevant agencies if there is interest.
MoHUA's role Convene inter-departmental coordination with MoEFCC, CPCB, and other relevant ministries to identify convergence points. Commission the national SWM digital portal under the SWM Rules 2026 mandate. If the SVANidhi heat support concept is of interest, facilitate conversations with relevant agencies.
eGov's support Share Karnataka SWM DPI architecture as a potential input to the national portal design. Support CPCB and SPCBs in designing the SWM-to-air-quality data pipeline. On SVANidhi heat support, facilitate early-stage design conversations and provide the SVANidhi data layer design inputs.
Platform layer Karnataka SWM platform (DIGIT-based) as architectural reference. CPCB ambient air quality monitoring data as the enforcement trigger source. SVANidhi vendor registry and digital payment rails as the disbursement layer. Satellite/sensor data and community field reporting for waste burning detection.
Policy layer SWM Rules 2026 — SPCB enforcement authority and 6-month national portal mandate. DRAP's September 2026 dumpsite remediation deadline. CPCB's national ambient air quality standards as the regulatory anchor. SVANidhi programme framework for the heat support pilot.
Why now → SWM Rules 2026 came into force April 1, 2026. The national portal mandate has a 6-month window. Karnataka SWM work is already live. DRAP's September 2026 deadline creates additional urgency on waste tracking infrastructure.
06
National RERA Data Standards Platform
eGov is already on the AIFORERA Technical Committee developing common data standards for state RERA platforms. The natural next step is supporting MoHUA in moving from standards to a unified national implementation.
▾
MoHUA and AIFORERA (All India Forum of Real Estate Regulatory Authorities) are developing a Unified RERA Dashboard and API-based platform connecting all state and UT RERAs. AIFORERA has constituted a Technical Committee of five RERA Chairpersons and invited eGov to support the development of common data standards for all state RERA platforms. The draft standards aim to ensure uniform implementation of the RERA Act and a consistent homebuyer experience across states, while providing MoHUA with sector-wide visibility through a common data architecture. The standards design is already underway. The logical next step, if MoHUA chooses to pursue it, is moving from standards to platform implementation.
MoHUA's role Lead the AIFORERA initiative as the apex ministry for RERA. Drive adoption of common data standards across state RERAs. Commission and own the Unified RERA Dashboard as the national homebuyer transparency and regulatory oversight tool.
eGov's support Continue Technical Committee participation on data standards. If MoHUA moves to platform implementation, provide technical support for the API integration layer connecting state RERA systems. DIGIT's interoperability architecture is directly applicable to a federated multi-state platform.
Platform layer API-based integration with all state RERA portals. Common data schema (under development) as the interoperability standard. Unified dashboard for MoHUA oversight, homebuyer transparency, and state-level benchmarking.
Policy layer RERA Act 2016 as the legal foundation. AIFORERA's mandate to drive uniform implementation. The common data standards currently being drafted as the technical policy instrument. MoHUA's oversight role provides the national coordination authority.
Why now → eGov is already on the AIFORERA Technical Committee — the standards work is live. The transition from standards to platform is the natural next step and can be proposed within the existing committee structure.
