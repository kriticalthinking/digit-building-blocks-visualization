Partnership Proposal · Urban India

Reviving and deepening
the eGov–MoHUA partnership

● Technical support to MoHUA's urban transformation agenda

eGov Foundation and MoHUA have a shared history that few partnerships in Indian digital governance can match. Working under MoHUA's leadership, eGov supported the architecture of DIGIT, which became the technical foundation for UPYOG — India's national urban governance platform, now fully owned and operated by MoHUA through NIUA, serving 4,400+ urban local bodies across 30 states.

The context for deepening this partnership is significant. The UCF operational guidelines were launched in April 2026, unlocking ₹4 lakh crore in urban investment on a reform-linked, challenge-mode basis. The 16th Finance Commission has committed ₹3.56 lakh crore to ULBs over 2026–31. eGov's PFM team is already engaged under MoHUA's direction on NMAM 2.0 and the XVI FC Digital Grants Management system.

The six ideas below are not about resting on past work. They are areas where eGov believes it can contribute technical capacity in support of MoHUA's priorities — on UCF programme infrastructure, UPYOG adoption, governance intelligence, citizen engagement, climate-resilient cities, and RERA data standards. In each case, MoHUA leads; eGov supports.

Six proposals

Where eGov can support
MoHUA's urban agenda

Each proposal identifies a gap in MoHUA's current digital infrastructure where eGov's existing work and technical capacity could be useful. In each case, MoHUA and its agencies (NIUA, AIFORERA, HUDCO) would lead and own the initiative. eGov's role is to provide technical support, platform capacity, and implementation assistance — on MoHUA's terms.

01
UCF Digital Operating System
The mission-management backbone MoHUA needs to actually run UCF at ₹4 lakh crore scale — reform tracking, tranche-linked fund release, and DPR quality infrastructure.
The UCF operational guidelines mandate a centralised reform portal where cities submit roadmaps, report progress, and trigger tranche releases — but this portal has no designated builder yet. Fund releases require geo-tagged physical progress evidence, utilisation certificates, and IRMA verification. At the scale of 4,223 eligible cities across three verticals and five reform tracks, this is a significant digital infrastructure challenge. eGov's ongoing NMAM 2.0 and XVI FC DGM work has produced directly relevant system design thinking that could feed into MoHUA's approach here.
MoHUA's role Lead the design and ownership of the UCF programme management system through the UCF division and NIUA. Define the reform portal architecture, NAC workflows, and IRMA coordination framework. Operate and own the system long-term.
eGov's support Provide technical inputs on system architecture drawing from NMAM 2.0 and DGM work — reform milestone schema, UC generation from transactional data, tranche-release workflow design. Available to embed a technical team within MoHUA's programme unit.
Platform layer DIGIT infrastructure as a potential foundation. PFMS integration for fund flow verification. APIs to UPYOG for service delivery data and cityfinance.in for financial account status.
Policy layer NMAM 2.0 accounting standards inform the data schema. UCF reform tracks (Sections 8.1–8.5 of guidelines) define the milestone framework. XVI FC grant conditions provide the compliance baseline.
Why now → UCF operational guidelines were published April 2026. The reform portal is referenced but unbuilt. States must submit project proposals within 6 months. eGov's DGM workshops (April 7–9) already produced system design thinking directly relevant to this.
02
UPYOG Adoption as UCF Reform Evidence
Define UPYOG operational status as the measurable benchmark for Digital Governance reform compliance under UCF — turning adoption from a push exercise into a national pull.
Digital Governance is an admissible UCF project category, explicitly including digitisation of property records, utilities, and family IDs. UCF's reform framework requires "end-to-end digitisation of select municipal functions" as a governance reform condition — but the reform toolkit, which will define this standard, is yet to be issued. MoHUA and NIUA are well-placed to define UPYOG operational status (property tax, water billing, trade licences, grievance redressal live with transaction volume) as the measurable benchmark for this condition. Cities that already have it get reform credit; cities that don't can use it as their UCF Digital Governance project funded through the PPCBF track. eGov can provide technical support in designing this maturity framework.
MoHUA's role Lead the design and notification of the Digital Governance Reform Index through the UCF reform toolkit. Embed UPYOG operational status in challenge-mode evaluation criteria at NAC. NIUA to coordinate with state teams on adoption pathways.
eGov's support Provide technical inputs on UPYOG module maturity levels (configured / live / transaction-generating / outcome-tracked). Support state onboarding through the 70-partner empanelled SI network. eGov Academy available for the individual capacity building the UCF guidelines require.
Platform layer UPYOG's 16 service modules as the implementation vehicle. DIGIT's open APIs allow state SIs to configure at scale. The maturity framework would be tracked within the UCF reform portal (Idea 1).
Policy layer UCF Section 8.2 (Governance & Digital Reforms) provides the policy hook. The UCF reform toolkit — to be issued separately per the guidelines — is the right vehicle for MoHUA to formalise this standard.
Why now → The UCF reform toolkit is explicitly listed as "to be issued separately." The window to shape what counts as digital governance reform is open now — once notified without this standard, it closes. A brief technical note from eGov to MoHUA/NIUA could seed this conversation.
03
National Urban Governance Intelligence Layer
A synthesis platform that pulls from UPYOG, cityfinance.in, AAINA, and the UCF reform portal to give MoHUA and cities an integrated governance health view that no existing system provides.
MoHUA currently has fragmented data systems covering different dimensions of city performance: cityfinance.in (financial accounts and rankings, built by Janaagraha), AAINA (self-reported service benchmarks), UPYOG (live transactional service delivery data), and the nascent UCF reform portal. No system synthesises across them. The challenge-mode evaluation for UCF requires MoHUA to assess cities across vision/capacity, transformative outcomes, and financial sustainability — each drawing on a different source. A governance intelligence layer that integrates these through APIs could give MoHUA, states, and cities a composite health view that doesn't exist today. This is not a competing portal — it would make existing portals more useful. A design conversation that includes Janaagraha (given their cityfinance.in ownership) would be the natural starting point. The prototype below illustrates what this could look like.
MoHUA's role Determine whether a ministry-level governance intelligence view is a priority. If so, lead the convening of a design group with NIUA, Janaagraha, and technical partners. Enable API access across UPYOG/NIUA, AAINA, and the UCF reform portal. Own the platform and its public transparency layer.
eGov's support Contribute technical architecture inputs and the UPYOG data pipeline design. Support the API integration work given eGov's existing relationship with NIUA. Facilitate a design conversation with Janaagraha to ensure cityfinance.in data is integrated, not replicated.
Platform layer API integrations: UPYOG (transactional service data) + cityfinance.in (financial accounts) + AAINA (benchmarks) + UCF portal (reform trajectory). No new data collection — synthesis and presentation only.
Policy layer UCF's challenge-mode evaluation framework (Sections 7.2 A/B/C) provides the natural assessment structure. A ministry-owned governance intelligence platform would make UCF evaluation evidence-based rather than reliant on self-reporting.
Why now → UCF's NAC will need to evaluate thousands of city proposals. MoHUA's budget has grown 50% to ₹85,522 crore. An integrated view of how that investment performs across cities would be valuable — and the data sources to build it already exist.
04
Ward-Level Citizen Engagement Layer
A structured digital citizen voice mechanism — multilingual, ward-mapped, linked to UPYOG service delivery data — closing the loop between what MoHUA sees and what citizens actually experience.
Every existing data system in the urban governance stack — UPYOG, AAINA, cityfinance.in — captures government-generated data. None systematically captures the citizen experience of service delivery at ward level. The UCF guidelines require community engagement and stakeholder consultations as eligible PPCBF activities. eGov has a working prototype of a ward-level citizen voice platform (developed for Bengaluru, multilingual) that captures grievances, service ratings, and local priority inputs — mapped to wards and cross-referenced with UPYOG service delivery records. If MoHUA sees value in a national citizen engagement standard for UCF, eGov can support the technical design and scaling of such a platform.
MoHUA's role Determine whether citizen engagement standards should be embedded in the UCF reform toolkit. If so, define what ward-level digital participation means as a governance reform indicator. Provide the policy framework for cities to adopt this as part of their UCF reform roadmap.
eGov's support Share the Bengaluru prototype for MoHUA/NIUA's review. If taken forward, support scaling to a multi-city, multilingual platform and build the API connecting citizen signals to the governance intelligence layer (Idea 3).
Platform layer Ward-mapped feedback interface (multilingual, mobile-first). Cross-referencing with UPYOG service transaction data. Optional ICCC integration for real-time grievance routing — consistent with UCF's mandatory ICCC requirement for all projects.
Policy layer UCF PPCBF covers community engagement activities. The 74th Constitutional Amendment mandates Ward Committees as participatory bodies — a digital citizen engagement platform operationalises that mandate. Citizen participation data could inform AAINA's benchmarking framework over time.
Why now → UCF explicitly funds community engagement under the ₹5,000 crore PPCBF. A working prototype already exists. The governance intelligence layer (Idea 3) benefits from a citizen data source. eGov can share the prototype with MoHUA/NIUA as a starting point for discussion.
05
Inter-Departmental Convergence for Climate-Resilient Cities
MoHUA as convener of cross-ministry digital infrastructure — connecting SWM, air quality enforcement, and urban livelihoods data to activate climate resilience at city scale.
UCF explicitly lists climate-resilient infrastructure as a project category. But the most impactful climate interventions in cities require data and regulatory authority that sits across multiple departments — SWM with ULBs, air quality with pollution control boards, livelihoods with MoLE. MoHUA is uniquely positioned to convene this convergence. Two early-stage activations illustrate the opportunity, both grounded in eGov's ongoing work:

SWM → Air Quality enforcement pipeline. Open waste burning is a significant source of PM2.5 in Indian cities. The Solid Waste Management Rules 2026 (in force from April 1, 2026) formally designate State Pollution Control Boards (e.g. KSPCB) as enforcement authorities for SWM — but there is currently no data pipeline connecting SWM operations to these boards. A centralised national digital waste tracking portal is mandated within 6 months under the new rules. eGov is already building SWM digital infrastructure on DIGIT with the Government of Karnataka (MoU with RDPR in place). This architecture — covering waste classification, route optimisation, MRF management, and citizen grievance — could feed directly into the national portal architecture MoHUA will need to commission. Connecting it to CPCB's ambient air quality monitoring data creates a traceable SWM-to-air-quality accountability chain that enforcement authorities currently lack.

Parametric heat insurance for PM SVANidhi vendors. ● early stage Street vendors — SVANidhi beneficiaries — are among the most exposed urban populations to extreme heat events. Parametric insurance products triggered by heat index thresholds (rather than assessed claims) are being explored by climate finance specialists as a way to provide rapid, verifiable income support during heat waves. eGov is in early-stage discussion with climate finance partners on this concept. MoHUA's existing SVANidhi digital infrastructure (vendor registration, geolocation, digital payment rails) provides a ready beneficiary identification and disbursement layer that such a product would need. If MoHUA were interested in piloting this, eGov could facilitate a design conversation between the ministry, potential insurance partners, and IRDAI.
MoHUA's role Convene the inter-departmental working group (MoHUA + MoEFCC + CPCB + MoLE) to identify convergence points. Commission the national SWM digital portal under the SWM Rules 2026 mandate. If the SVANidhi heat insurance pilot is of interest, facilitate conversations with IRDAI and finance partners.
eGov's support Share Karnataka SWM DPI architecture as a potential input to the national portal design. Support CPCB and KSPCB in designing the SWM-to-air-quality data pipeline. On SVANidhi heat insurance, facilitate early-stage design conversations and provide the SVANidhi data layer design support.
Platform layer Karnataka SWM platform (DIGIT-based) as architectural reference. CPCB's ambient air quality monitoring data as the enforcement trigger source. SVANidhi vendor registry and digital payment rails as the insurance disbursement layer. Satellite / sensor data and community field reporting for waste burning detection.
Policy layer SWM Rules 2026 — SPCB enforcement authority and 6-month national portal mandate. UCF Water & Sanitation and Climate Resilience verticals for funding pathway. SVANidhi programme framework for the heat insurance pilot. CPCB's national ambient air quality standards as the regulatory anchor.
Why now → SWM Rules 2026 came into force April 1, 2026. The national portal mandate has a 6-month window. Karnataka SWM work is already live. DRAP's Sept 2026 deadline creates additional urgency on the waste tracking infrastructure. The convergence opportunity is time-bound.
06
National RERA Data Standards Platform
Building on eGov's existing role on the AIFORERA Technical Committee — from common data standards to a unified national RERA platform that gives homebuyers, states, and MoHUA consistent visibility.
MoHUA and AIFORERA (All India Forum of Real Estate Regulatory Authorities) are developing a Unified RERA Dashboard and API-based platform connecting all state and UT RERAs. AIFORERA has constituted a Technical Committee of five RERA Chairpersons and invited eGov to support the development of common data standards for all state RERA platforms. The draft standards aim to ensure uniform implementation of the RERA Act and a consistent homebuyer experience across states, while providing MoHUA with sector-wide visibility through a common data architecture. The foundation work — standards design — is already underway. The logical next step, if MoHUA chooses to pursue it, is moving from standards to platform implementation.
MoHUA's role Lead the AIFORERA initiative as the apex ministry for RERA. Drive adoption of common data standards across state RERAs. Commission and own the Unified RERA Dashboard as the national homebuyer transparency and regulatory oversight tool.
eGov's support Continue technical committee participation on data standards. If MoHUA moves to platform implementation, provide technical support for the API integration layer connecting state RERA systems. DIGIT's interoperability architecture is directly applicable to a federated multi-state platform of this kind.
Platform layer API-based integration with all state RERA portals. Common data schema (under development) as the interoperability standard. Unified dashboard for MoHUA oversight, homebuyer transparency, and state-level benchmarking. DIGIT's federated architecture as a potential implementation reference.
Policy layer RERA Act 2016 as the legal foundation. AIFORERA's mandate to drive uniform implementation. The common data standards (currently being drafted with eGov's support) as the technical policy instrument. MoHUA's oversight role provides the national coordination authority.
Why now → eGov is already on the AIFORERA Technical Committee — the standards work is live. The transition from standards to platform is the natural next step and can be proposed without needing to establish a new relationship. This is the most immediately actionable idea on the page.
