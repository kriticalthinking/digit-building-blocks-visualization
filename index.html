The problem

Governments build the same digital infrastructure. Repeatedly. In silos.

Every government that delivers public services — property tax, health campaigns, water connections, social benefits — needs the same underlying digital capabilities: registries, workflows, identity verification, payments, notifications. Today, most build these independently, at significant cost, with limited interoperability and frequent dependency on proprietary vendors. The scale of this gap is measurable: only 39% of citizens in surveyed countries trust their government to deliver services reliably (OECD, 2023), fewer than 1 in 3 countries are on track for accountable public institutions by 2030 (UN SDG 16.6), and over $1 trillion is lost annually to fragmented, inefficient public administration (World Bank, 2022). We need to collectively reimagine and deliver what good public services can look like — and the building blocks approach is one proven path.

The consequence of the current approach
⟶  Duplicated investment across agencies and governments
⟶  Fragmented data with no single source of truth
⟶  Proprietary systems that governments cannot own or modify
⟶  Citizens navigating disconnected services
The building blocks alternative
✓  Shared, reusable infrastructure across services and sectors
✓  Government-owned, open-source, no vendor dependency
✓  Modular, deploy what you need, configure for your context
✓  Proven at population scale across multiple countries

The solution

Digital building blocks that help governments improve public service delivery across sectors.

Digital infrastructure alone does not transform public service delivery. Governments that have achieved sustained impact at scale have worked across all three layers simultaneously, investing in the enabling conditions, deploying shared digital infrastructure, and measuring outcomes for citizens. Technology that arrives without policy, institutional capacity, or community trust rarely delivers on its promise.

How eGov works

Five modes of engagement. One coherent mission.

eGov Foundation does not have a single fixed role. Depending on where a government or partner is in their digital transformation journey, eGov steps in differently — as strategist, platform provider, implementation partner, capacity builder, or ecosystem architect. These five modes are not mutually exclusive: a single state engagement typically involves several, sequenced over time. Together they form the coherent India strategy that has made DIGIT one of the most widely deployed open-source governance platforms in the world.

The impact

What this approach has unlocked for India.

India offers some of the most extensive evidence available for what the building blocks approach can achieve at population scale, across local governance, public health, water, sanitation, and public finance. The outcomes documented here belong to the governments that led and now own these systems. eGov Foundation has been a consistent technical and institutional partner across many of these programmes, contributing open digital infrastructure, programme architecture, and capacity building.


These impact stories are drawn from published government sources. They illustrate how the same underlying building blocks have been configured differently across sectors, scales, and institutional contexts.

UPYOG, India's National Urban Governance Platform

In February 2021, MoHUA and MEITY jointly launched the National Urban Digital Mission (NUDM), anchored at NIUA. NUDM partnered with eGov Foundation to revamp and scale DIGIT, which became the technical foundation for UPYOG, the Urban Platform for deliverY of Online Governance. eGov's contribution spanned programme design, architecture, change management, capacity building, and over 20,000 hours of training. UPYOG is now fully owned and operated by MoHUA through NIUA, certified as a Digital Public Good by the DPGA, and recipient of the Gold Award at the ETGovernment DigiTech Awards 2025.

Our approach

Building blocks designed to solve multiple governance needs, delivering better public services for citizens.

The building blocks approach treats digital governance infrastructure the way we treat roads or electricity grids, as shared public goods that any service can utilise, rather than systems independently constructed for each application. DIGIT offers twenty-four such blocks across six functional areas. Governments configure and combine them for their specific context, retaining full ownership and control.

The enablers

Sustained digital transformation needs more than technology.

Experience across countries and contexts shows that the most significant barriers to effective public service delivery are rarely technical. They are institutional, legal, social, and human. These enabling conditions must be developed alongside, and often prior to, any digital investment.

◎
Digital transformation is not primarily a technology problem. Governments and support organisations that have achieved sustained impact consistently report that institutional arrangements, legal frameworks, data governance, community trust, and human capacity are the decisive factors. Each of the conditions below represents an area requiring deliberate, multi-stakeholder investment, and each one is as important as the technology itself. About eGov Foundation

How eGov works with government partners.

eGov Foundation is a non-profit organisation founded in 2003, working at the intersection of open digital infrastructure and governance reform. eGov does not sell software licences and does not earn revenue from the use of DIGIT, the platform is a certified Digital Public Good, available to any government at no cost. eGov's role is to help governments succeed with this approach, from early strategy through to long-term institutionalisation, and then step back.

"When the engagement ends, state officials own the knowledge and the digital infrastructure, not eGov." - eGov Foundation's operating principle across all government partnerships
